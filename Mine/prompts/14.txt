
## Letter 14: 20th Century Philosophy

*From: Dr. Marcus Chen*
*To: Dr. Eleanor Vance*

My dear Eleanor,

Your question about the relationship between philosophical developments and their cultural context is particularly relevant to the 20th century, which saw unprecedented changes in science, technology, politics, and art that profoundly influenced philosophy.

**Francis Herbert Bradley** developed **absolute idealism** in Britain, arguing that reality is a single, all-encompassing experience. He criticized the notion of relations as real, maintaining that only the absolute whole is truly real. This idealist tradition would soon be challenged by analytic philosophy.

**Gottlob Frege** revolutionized logic through his **predicate calculus**, which allowed for more complex logical reasoning than Aristotle's syllogistic logic. He distinguished between **sense** (Sinn) and **reference** (Bedeutung), arguing that expressions have both meaning (the way they present their object) and reference (the object they refer to).

Frege's logicism—the view that mathematics is reducible to logic—aimed to provide secure foundations for mathematics. His work was largely overlooked until Bertrand Russell recognized its significance.

**Henri Poincaré** developed **conventionalism**, arguing that some principles of science are conventions chosen for convenience rather than truths discovered about nature. He emphasized the role of **hypothesis** in science and the underdetermination of theory by evidence.

**Sigmund Freud** created **psychoanalysis**, arguing that unconscious drives and conflicts shape human behavior. His theory of the unconscious, repression, and the interpretation of dreams revolutionized our understanding of the mind. Freud's approach to the self as divided rather than unified challenged traditional philosophical conceptions of rational agency.

In India, while Freud was developing psychoanalysis, **Sri Aurobindo** was developing his **integral yoga**, which also recognized unconscious aspects of human nature but emphasized spiritual evolution rather than psychic conflict.

**Ferdinand de Saussure** founded **structuralism** in linguistics, arguing that language is a system of signs where meaning arises from differences between signs rather than from reference to external objects. His distinction between **langue** (the language system) and **parole** (individual speech acts) influenced structuralism and poststructuralism.

**Edmund Husserl** founded **phenomenology**, attempting to establish philosophy as a rigorous science by returning **"to the things themselves"**—to experience as it is given, prior to theoretical interpretations. His method of **epoché** (bracketing) suspends judgment about the external world to focus on the structure of consciousness.

Husserl's phenomenology influenced both existentialism and analytic philosophy, particularly his emphasis on intentionality and the lifeworld (Lebenswelt).

**Henri Bergson** developed a philosophy of **duration** (la durée), arguing that time is not a series of discrete moments but a continuous flow. He criticized the spatialization of time in science and philosophy, emphasizing intuition as a way of grasping duration directly.

Bergson's emphasis on creative evolution and élan vital (vital force) as driving life's development offered an alternative to mechanistic explanations of life. His philosophy resonates with **process philosophy** and **Daoist** emphasis on flow and change.

**John Dewey** developed **pragmatism** into **instrumentalism**, viewing concepts as tools for solving problems rather than descriptions of reality. He emphasized **democracy** as an ethical way of life and **education** as the foundation of democratic society.

Dewey's philosophy of education emphasized learning through experience and problem-solving rather than rote memorization. His democratic idealism contrasted with the more pessimistic views of many European philosophers.

**Pierre Duhem** emphasized the **holistic nature of scientific confirmation**, arguing that we test theories as whole networks rather than in isolation. His **Duhem-Quine thesis** would later influence philosophy of science.

**George Santayana** developed a **materialist** philosophy that recognized the spiritual dimensions of human life. His famous dictum "Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it" emphasized the importance of historical consciousness.

**John Ellis McTaggart** argued for the **unreality of time**, distinguishing between A-series (past, present, future) and B-series (earlier, later) conceptions of time and arguing that both are incoherent, leading to the conclusion that time is unreal.

**Benedetto Croce** developed **idealism** in Italy, arguing that reality is spiritual activity and that history is the story of liberty. He distinguished between the four aspects of spirit: the theoretical (aesthetic and logical) and the practical (economic and ethical).

**Clive Bell** introduced the concept of **significant form** in aesthetics, arguing that the quality that makes art art is not representation but the organization of lines and colors that provokes an aesthetic emotion.

**Hans Kelsen** developed the **pure theory of law**, arguing that legal systems should be analyzed as hierarchies of norms without reference to moral or social considerations. His **Grundnorm** (basic norm) concept attempted to explain the validity of legal systems.

**George Edward Moore** defended **common sense** against philosophical skepticism, arguing that we know many things with certainty (e.g., "Here is a hand") and that philosophical arguments that contradict common sense must be mistaken. His **open question argument** challenged attempts to define "good" in natural terms.

This period saw increasing specialization in philosophy, with distinct movements emerging. How do you think this specialization affected philosophy's ability to address the big questions?

With philosophical curiosity,
Marcus
