
## Letter 7: Roman Philosophy and Neoplatonism

*From: Dr. Eleanor Vance*
*To: Dr. Marcus Chen*

Dear Marcus,

Your question about which Hellenistic approach is most compelling is difficult—they each offer valuable insights. Perhaps the wisest path combines elements of each: Epicurean moderation in pleasures, Stoic acceptance of what we can't control, Pyrrhonian humility about our knowledge, and Cynic authenticity in living according to nature.

As Greek culture spread to Rome, these philosophical schools evolved. **Timon of Phlius**, a follower of Pyrrho, continued the skeptical tradition, arguing against dogmatism in all its forms. His satirical critiques of other philosophers remind me of **Zhuangzi's** playful undermining of philosophical positions in China.

**Epictetus**, a Stoic philosopher who was born a slave, emphasized the distinction between what depends on us and what doesn't. His **Enchiridion** (Handbook) begins: "Some things are in our control and others not." This practical approach to Stoicism influenced countless people seeking wisdom for living well.

The Roman emperor **Marcus Aurelius** was perhaps the most famous Stoic practitioner. His **Meditations**, written in Greek as personal reflections, reveal a mind struggling to apply Stoic principles to the challenges of ruling an empire and facing personal loss. His emphasis on accepting the present moment and fulfilling one's duties resonates with **Buddhist mindfulness** and **Confucian** concepts of proper role-fulfillment.

The most significant philosophical development in late antiquity was **Neoplatonism**, founded by **Plotinus**. He reinterpreted Plato's philosophy, emphasizing the **One** as an utterly simple, transcendent principle beyond being itself. From the One emanates **Nous** (Intellect), which contains the Platonic Forms, and from Nous emanates the **World Soul**, which creates the material world.

Plotinus described the soul's journey as a return to the One through contemplation and purification. This mystical philosophy had profound influence on later religious thought and bears striking similarities to **Advaita Vedanta** in India, particularly the concept of **Brahman** as the ultimate reality beyond all distinctions.

The Neoplatonic emphasis on mystical union with the divine contrasts with the more practical ethics of the Stoics and Epicureans. Plotinus saw philosophy not merely as a way to live well but as a path to union with ultimate reality.

As the classical world gave way to the Christian era, philosophy would increasingly serve theology. **Saint Augustine** converted to Christianity after being influenced by Neoplatonism, particularly Plotinus' emphasis on the immaterial and transcendent. Augustine's **Confessions** represents a new kind of philosophical writing—personal, introspective, and explicitly religious.

Augustine adapted Platonic ideas to Christian doctrine, arguing that eternal truths exist in the mind of God rather than in a separate realm of Forms. His doctrine of **divine illumination**—that God illuminates our minds to know truth—transformed epistemology.

In China, as Christianity was spreading in the West, **Buddhism** was being transformed through its encounter with **Daoism**, giving rise to **Chan** (Zen) Buddhism. Both Augustine and early Chan masters were adapting existing philosophical traditions to new religious insights.

This period saw philosophy increasingly subordinated to theology in the West, while in the East, philosophy and religion had long been more integrated. How do you think this relationship between philosophy and religion shaped subsequent thought?

Yours in historical reflection,
Eleanor
